# Best Friends Story Outline

## Themes

- Lifelong friendship as an active choice, not a magical cure.
- Recovery as uneven: progress, setbacks, shame, anger, exhaustion, and hope can coexist.
- Support as presence: the protagonist cannot fix Sayori, but he can stay, listen, respect boundaries, and help connect her to adults and professionals.
- The Literature Club as a changed community: each member reacts imperfectly and grows through accountability.
- Meta remnants as second-chance echoes rather than a new doom spiral.

## Act Structure

### Act I — Festival Weather

The mod opens near the festival preparations. Sayori is cheerful but increasingly late, forgetful, and self-deprecating. The protagonist notices small changes but explains them away until Sayori admits that she has felt rainclouds for years.

### Act II — The Morning That Changes

On festival morning, the protagonist wakes with dread and rushes to Sayori's house instead of waiting. He finds that Sayori has attempted suicide, intervenes without procedural detail, calls emergency services, and rides the first wave of shock as she is taken to the hospital.

### Act III — Aftermath

The festival is canceled. Sayori survives and is hospitalized. She feels guilty for scaring everyone. The protagonist struggles with intrusive memories and fear of leaving her alone. Natsuki lashes out because anger is easier than terror. Yuri blames herself for missing signs. Monika has flashes of another reality where the rescue did not happen and quietly resolves not to waste this chance.

### Act IV — Learning to Stay

Weeks pass. Sayori returns home with a safety plan, therapy appointments, and fragile boundaries. The club learns that support is not surveillance or forced happiness. Some days are warm; others are frighteningly quiet. The protagonist and Sayori remain best friends first, allowing any romantic feelings to remain tentative and optional.

### Act V — The Delayed Festival

Months later, the club finally holds the festival they canceled. The event is smaller and more honest than the original plan. Sayori reads a poem about fearing tomorrow but choosing to meet it anyway. The ending is bittersweet: she is not cured, the protagonist is not unscarred, and Monika still hears faint echoes, but no one is alone.

## Meta Element Guidelines

- Use brief corrupted text and déjà vu sparingly.
- Monika's memories should make her protective, not controlling.
- Glitches should underline the fragility of the second chance.
- Avoid turning Sayori's survival into another shock twist.

## Safety and Tone Guidelines

- Do not describe suicide methods in instructional or procedural detail.
- Show emergency response, hospitalization, therapy, and adult support as necessary and normal.
- Let characters make mistakes, apologize, and change.
- Avoid implying love alone cures depression.
